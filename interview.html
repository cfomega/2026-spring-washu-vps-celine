Interview: Bridging Imagination to Technicality

The Art within Accounting

Q: Please introduce yourself, your field of work, and your relationship with creativity and AI as a whole?

A: Hi, my name is Rini. I'm a professor of accounting. I conduct accounting research and teach both undergraduate and graduate-level courses. These courses train students to prepare financial reports, interpret accounting information, and communicate it in ways that help organizations make better business decisions. While accounting is often taught as a technical or rule-based discipline, I see it as a field that requires both analytical thinking and creativity. The relationship between accounting and creativity comes from translating complex financial data into meaningful insights. Numbers alone do not tell a story—someone has to interpret them, identify patterns, and present them clearly. Artificial intelligence has become an increasingly important tool in this process. AI can process large datasets, detect patterns, and automate repetitive tasks. However, human judgment, interpretation, and communication remain essential. AI allows accountants to spend less time on mechanical tasks and more time on creative analysis.

Q: Do you think accounting has elements of creativity similar to art or design? Where do you see it most?

A: Yes, accounting definitely contains elements of creativity. It appears most clearly in interpretation, problem-solving, and communication. Financial data rarely speaks for itself—accountants must interpret it within a business context. Creativity is also present in designing financial reports, dashboards, and models. These are essentially visual narratives about an organization’s performance. Choosing what to highlight and how to structure information requires thoughtful design. Additionally, creativity plays a role in strategic thinking, such as pricing, cost management, and investment analysis, where professionals must imagine different scenarios and outcomes.

Q: How can accountants think more like artists when interpreting or presenting financial information?

" Accountants can think more like artists by focusing on storytelling."

A: Instead of just presenting numbers, they should ask what story the data is telling. They can also apply design thinking—prioritizing clarity, balance, and focus when building reports or dashboards. Highlighting key insights rather than overwhelming users with data is crucial. Finally, experimentation is important. Trying new visualization tools, interactive dashboards, and modeling techniques can make financial information more engaging and accessible.

Q: Have you seen examples where creative thinking changed how financial data was communicated or understood?

A: Yes, a clear example is the use of data visualization. Traditional reports with tables of numbers can be difficult to interpret, but charts and dashboards make insights easier to understand. Another example is narrative reporting, where financial data is combined with explanations and visuals. This helps decision-makers understand not just what happened, but why it happened.

Q: How important is visual design when presenting financial information?

A: Visual design is extremely important. Decision-makers often have limited time, so clear visuals help highlight trends and relationships quickly. Poor design can lead to confusion, while effective design improves understanding and efficiency. It is not just about aesthetics—it directly impacts how accurately information is interpreted.

Q: How does accounting benefit from principles used in art and graphic design?

A: Design principles like composition, color, and clarity are very valuable. Composition helps organize information logically, color can highlight performance (such as green for positive and red for concern), and clarity ensures the message is easy to understand. These principles help users navigate and interpret financial data more effectively.

Q: What makes a financial report both accurate and aesthetically effective?

"Accuracy is the foundation; data must be correct and reliable. "

A: Aesthetic effectiveness comes from clear and thoughtful presentation. A strong report organizes information logically, highlights key metrics, and uses visuals to guide interpretation. When accuracy and design work together, reports become powerful decision-making tools.

Q: What are the benefits and challenges of AI in accounting? How will creativity become more important as AI advances, and where will human creativity remain essential?

A: AI can automate repetitive tasks, analyze large datasets, and detect patterns quickly. This allows accountants to focus more on strategy and interpretation. However, challenges include over-reliance on AI, lack of transparency, and potential data bias. These issues are similar to those faced in creative fields using AI. And yes, creativity will become even more important. As AI handles routine tasks, human value will lie in interpretation, communication, and strategic thinking. Human creativity is essential in judgment, context, communication, and ethics. These areas require understanding beyond what AI can replicate.

Q: What is ethical vs. unethical AI use in accounting?

"Ethical use involves transparency, accuracy, and accountability."

A: Professionals should verify AI outputs and disclose usage. Unethical use includes manipulating results, relying blindly on AI, or violating data privacy.

Q: How can accountants make financial insights more intuitive?

A: By focusing on storytelling, relevance, and visualization. Connecting numbers to real-world impact makes insights more meaningful and easier to understand.

Q: Should accounting be taught more like a creative discipline? How would the profession change if accounting were treated as a creative field?

A: Yes, combining technical skills with creativity would better prepare students. This includes storytelling, visualization, and real-world problem-solving.Accountants would become more like strategic advisors, focusing on communication, visualization, and insight. The field would become more collaborative and interdisciplinary.

Q: What advice do you have for adapting to AI?

A: Treat AI as a tool, not a replacement. Develop both technical skills and human qualities like creativity, empathy, and judgment.
