Reading Response: The Eno Documentary

At the start of the “Eno” documentary, Brian Eno states that whenever he creates art, “it’s happening and I’m happening with it.” I found his perspective fascinating, treating art as his own world to encompass the feelings he has poured into it and allowing it to become a separate entity that encapsulates a point in time. His inspiration of nature and evolution also incorporates how art can grow over time through loops that change every iteration or generative technology. It implies that variation or progression is an imitation of life, essential to human experience, where we are forced to live in different phases as the circumstances around us shift throughout our years.

Eno focuses on three things: a specific mix of blue and red, Kettie Lester’s deep voice, and long walks along the promenade during sunset. He showcases deep reflections in the things that move him, discovering how feelings are the beginning of everything; it is only a matter of articulating them. He had these thoughts in mind when creating music, but throughout his journey, he faced pressure from the music industry and was not always certain about where his creativity should be. Experimenting through creative slumps taught him that a feeling at a certain time, though it can inspire something, does not equate to how a piece of work stands on its own and exists in the fullest of time. I resonated with his words and found comfort that even prodigies can be afraid of the creative process; we all must find bravery in trusting ourselves with our visions.

Eno does not only reflect on the music itself. He extends to the community it brings and the representation it has of others. Answering “Why do we like music?” is difficult, but for Eno, synchronized chords mean just as much as synchronizing with others and the thrill of being brought together by experiencing the same sound, despite being impacted in different ways. He also points out the experience of being a singer connecting to others. The voice is a bold standing, embodying someone who stands on their own and leaving the crowd to wonder: “Who is the person singing this song?” or “How does their story connect with me?” Again, music becomes its separate being through the different people singers can become and the stories they tell.

The documentary goes further into how artists like Eno innovate. Eno’s band wrote on cards and played a game where the card determined how they should be in the studio. It allowed them to experiment as different people and drop their usual styles and biases when creating. Eno also states that it is important to distance your child-self, who is free to play with art, and the critic, who may shut down too many seeds of a great idea if introduced too early on. It was a great reminder to allow us to make mistakes and embrace that every idea could be the answer to a greater piece of work.

Overall, I thoroughly enjoyed the documentary’s art style. It featured a series of video cuts and transition music that encapsulated jumping through different times in Eno’s life while showcasing his bright mind. Although many images and patterns flashed by quickly and seemed random, the imagery was intentional through depictions of the world, nature, and moments where it was slowed down to appreciate the imagery and how it makes us feel (such as the animated color palette, sunlit liquid patterns, and retro designs such as spirals). These are all things related to some of Eno’s works and to the sources that inspired them. It was a great example of how a combination of things and how they are put together can represent someone, which is something I would like to learn how to do as I design my class website.
