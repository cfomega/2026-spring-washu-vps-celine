Reading Response: The Coming Age of Calm Technology (1996)

The article offered great insight into how technology has evolved over the last 50 years. I enjoyed following how the human relationship with technology has shaped through the phases outlined in the article. The progression of each helps to conceptualize how every phase has built up to the current Calm Technology era. I am impressed with how the essay dates back to 1996, yet the predictions and observations are still relevant today and build from the single computer, personal computer, and shared internet backgrounds.

Calm technology by itself is an interest threshold for designers. The article describes how compared to the previous eras, Calm Technology's value comes from the ability to switch between peripheral and centralized experiences. I remember having previous class discussions where design aids in technology by creating an immersive space where the focus becomes less about the wires, screens, and digitalized components, and more about the messaging or deeper feelings that the creator aims to bring out of the software. The relationship manifests out of technologies that takes a form outside of the typical screen, still using software but extending into a higher form of tangibility, which exists through smart home appliances and wearables nowadays. Although they did not exist when the article was written, they meet the same standards when compared to the article's examples of office windows and multicast video streams. They can sit in the background and be peripheral, but can also become central when the user needs to interact with them. Their nature also allows people to be more comfortable with hardware that do not appear overly techy or overwhelming. The focus is not being overly demanded in ways that limits deeper engagement, which are guidelines that should be kept in mind when desigining programs that mends the gap between advanced technological ability and the human connection to understanding and working with them.
