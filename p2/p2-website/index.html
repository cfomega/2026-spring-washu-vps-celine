PREFACE

MALCOLM ROGERS

During the closing decades of the nineteenth century, Japan's leaders strove to establish their country as an important member of the global community through an ambitious modernization program. A national psotal system was introduced in the 1870s and by the first few years of the 1900s the postcard had became a highly popular graphic medium. Its novelty, intimacy and immediacy inspired many well known Japanese artists to create innovative compositions that often synthesized new Western and traditional Japanese sensibilities. From early in its history the Museum of Fin Arts has been committed to telling the story of the art of Japan, and its extensive Japanese holdings include more than sixty thousand woodblock prints from the seventeenth through the nineteenth centuries. Although these prints are widely considered to form the most comprehensive collection of its kind in the world, great museums must never rest on their laurels, and it is incumbent upon us to seek out and present previously overlooked fields of artistic endeavor. Such an undertaking is impossible to accomplish alone. New committments by collectors and donors permit the Museum to convey the increasingly complex history of the world's art from the earliest times of our own. Leanord Lauder has made an invaluable contribution to that process Although perhaps best known as a collector of modern paintings, since childhood he has had an abiding passion for postcards, acquiring them form all over the wolrd because he blelieves not only that they are of historical and sociological interest, but more importantly that they are objects of great beauty. Acting on his conviction that it is the responsibility of a collector to share works of art with others, in the spring of 2002 Mr. Lauder made a gift of his remarkable collection of Japanese postccards to the Museum of Fin Arts. He decided that the institution would be an appropriate home for these works because he knew that they would "add strength to strength." Indeed, his donation has added an essential chapter to the story of Japanese printed art at the Museum.

INTRO: ART OF THE JAPANESE POSTCARD

An introductory essay on how postcards reflect Japan's rapid modernization. Postcards became a new traveling form of art that replaced woodblock prints and enabled communication. Japanese artists also experimented with non-Japanese styles of art through postcard making.
