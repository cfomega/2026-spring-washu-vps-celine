Writing Exercises: A Fulfilling Ritual/Routine

In 2026, I started journaling. Funnily enough, a lot of people have taken up journaling due to the aesthetics, notebooks, and stationery, which have filled up a lot of my For You Pages on social media. I’ll admit. Initially, I fell into the trap of being influenced and did it because it was a New Year’s trend, but as someone who needed an outlet and had been using my iPad to write in my courses, it was a good push into doing something that I had always wanted to do.

In 2024, I started to take my mental well-being a bit more seriously. I have always wanted something personal and nonjudgmental if friends, family, or counselors weren’t around or if I was in one of those slumps where I felt that I couldn’t trust someone else with how I was feeling in that moment. Therefore, journaling was the answer for me, and instead of pressuring myself to write in font-like handwriting, buy the stickers, trace the layouts, or think hard about the color of my pen, I treated my journal as a diary rather than a commercialized piece of art that I made for the online world.

It’s not that there’s anything wrong with that; such promotion is what got me to do something I needed. But I realized that I held myself back from truly expressing myself if I acted like I was ready to say my personal feelings to an audience. It was nice to just scratch in a plain notebook and not think about whiteout or how pretty it looks. The value of it really comes from it being a direct line of my unfiltered thoughts, and it helped to validate anything I was feeling rather than loop and loop my doubts.

Now, I specifically chose to do this on paper rather than an iPad, because it felt more tangible and grounded. It was important to do this with a traditional pen and paper, rather than a screen or typeface. 1) The pen was so that I wasn’t afraid of making mistakes, or that it wasn’t as easy to take something back. Sometimes I’m too much of a perfectionist, and that’s part of my struggles. So, penmanship is a small way of breaking that streak. 2) Writing engages my brain in ways that typing doesn’t. I really have to think about what I say and the drag of the paper slows me down so that I can really process everything. These things fulfill me because they give me structure when I am feeling my best or my worst. I can capture it all in one place and reflect on the things that happen in my life. It also allows my voice to grow and develop, and really feel everything in its entire rawness without shame. It’s my safe space and adds more security into my day and a source of comfort since I have let everything out.
